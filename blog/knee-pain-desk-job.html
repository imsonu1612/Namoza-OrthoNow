Do Stress And Anxiety Actually Cause Your Hip Pain?

RM
Dr. Rajesh Malhotra MBBS, Orthopaedic · AIIMS, New Delhi
8 min read · Published 16 May 2026

The pain in the hips that a definite structural pathology cannot explain is more common than many people may think, and more often than not, the cause lies outside the joint itself. The effects of stress and anxiety are measurable in physical changes in the musculoskeletal system. Among patients presenting at our clinic with persistent hip pain but unremarkable imaging, the mind-body connection is often the missing element of the diagnostic picture.

How Stress Physically Affects Your Hips

Psychological stress does not remain in your head. When the body perceives a threat — whether a deadline, a difficult relationship, or chronic anxiety — the nervous system initiates a sustained stress response. Cortisol and adrenaline flood the system, muscles tighten reflexively, and the body braces as though it were about to absorb an impact.

One of the most stress-reactive muscle groups in the body is the hip flexors, and in particular the iliopsoas muscle. The iliopsoas links the lumbar spine to the femur and lies adjacent to the diaphragm, making it acutely sensitive to the changes in breathing pattern that accompany chronic anxiety. Chronic tension in this muscle produces groin and hip pain that is, on physical examination, structurally identical to mechanical hip pathology.

Central Sensitisation: When the Pain System Gets Stuck

Beyond muscular tension, chronic psychological stress contributes to a phenomenon known as central sensitisation — a state in which the central nervous system becomes hypersensitised and amplifies pain signals disproportionate to any actual tissue damage.

This is not fabricated pain. The neurological pathways are well established and the pain is entirely real. The course of treatment, however, is fundamentally different from structural hip conditions, and conflating the two leads to interventions that do not help and sometimes cause harm.

When to See an Orthopaedic Doctor

Hip pain related to stress does not mean the pain is being dismissed — it means the correct workup is essential. Certain features always warrant a structural assessment regardless of the psychological context:

  • Pain following a fall or direct impact
  • Groin pain with restricted internal rotation — a consistent sign of hip joint pathology
  • Night pain that wakes you from sleep
  • Hip pain accompanied by fever or unexplained weight loss
  • Progressive worsening despite rest and stress management

An experienced orthopaedic doctor will rule out structural causes before attributing pain to psychosomatic factors. Imaging and clinical examination are both necessary. Stress does not exclude structural pathology and the two frequently co-exist.

Treatment When Stress Is the Primary Driver

In the absence of structural causes, the most effective approach combines several elements:

Targeted physiotherapy

Specific stretching and myofascial release directed at the iliopsoas will reduce pain in most patients within four to six weeks. The muscular tension causing the pain is physically real irrespective of its origin, and responds to physical treatment.

Breathing and nervous system regulation

Diaphragmatic breathing directly reduces iliopsoas hypertonicity due to the anatomical proximity of these structures. This is not a general wellness recommendation — it is a specific intervention with a clear mechanism.

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

CBT is a first-line intervention for chronic pain with a central sensitisation component. The evidence shows significant reduction in pain intensity and meaningful improvement in daily functioning. It does not require the patient to believe the pain is psychological for it to work.

Sleep optimisation

Poor sleep measurably increases pain sensitivity. A treatment plan that does not address sleep quality will underperform regardless of the other interventions in place.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can anxiety cause hip pain on one side only?
Yes, unilateral hip pain is typical in stress-related presentations. It often reflects habitual postural asymmetry or dominant-side muscle guarding that develops under chronic stress.
How do I know if my hip pain is serious?
Clinical assessment is necessary for any hip pain accompanied by restricted range of motion, palpable tenderness in the groin, or pain following trauma. Do not self-diagnose stress-related causes without first ruling out structural pathology.
Will physiotherapy help if my hip pain is stress-related?
Yes. Physiotherapy directed at the iliopsoas and surrounding hip flexor complex produces significant relief even when pain has a psychological basis, because the muscular tension generating the pain is physically real regardless of how it originated.

Hip pain warrants careful assessment, not guesswork. A full clinical evaluation and a treatment plan that accounts for the complete picture — structural and functional — is the correct starting point.
