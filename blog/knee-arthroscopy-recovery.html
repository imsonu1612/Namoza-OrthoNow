Returning To Activity: A Realistic 6-Month Recovery Timeline After Knee Arthroscopy

RM
Dr. Rajesh Malhotra MBBS, Orthopaedic · AIIMS, New Delhi
7 min read · Published 05 May 2026

One of the most common orthopaedic procedures — minimally invasive, highly effective, and far less disruptive than open knee surgery — is knee arthroscopy. However, the road to recovery is seldom a straight one. The question patients who come to our clinic after undergoing knee arthroscopy surgery in Delhi always ask is: "When can I get back to normal?" The honest answer depends on what was done inside the joint and how seriously you take rehabilitation.

What Is Knee Arthroscopy?

Knee arthroscopy is a keyhole procedure in which a small camera (arthroscope) is inserted into the knee joint through tiny incisions. It allows the surgeon to diagnose and treat a range of conditions — including meniscal tears, ligament injuries, cartilage damage, loose bodies, and synovitis — without opening the knee completely.

Most procedures take between 30 and 60 minutes and are carried out under regional or general anaesthesia. The majority of patients are discharged the same day.

Month 1: Control, Rest, and Early Movement

Weeks 1–2

The first two weeks focus on managing swelling and protecting the repair. Expect:

  • Significant swelling and stiffness — both are normal
  • Crutches for support, particularly after meniscal or ligament repair
  • Ice application for 20 minutes every 2–3 hours to reduce inflammation
  • Gentle range-of-motion exercises prescribed by your physiotherapist from Day 2–3

Weeks 3–4

Most patients are able to bear full weight and begin straight-leg strengthening exercises. Driving is usually possible at three to four weeks, provided it is your right knee and you are not taking opioid pain medication.

Month 2: Building Strength

By week six, swelling has typically reduced considerably. The focus now shifts to restoring quadriceps and hamstring strength — muscles that atrophy quickly following any knee surgery.

Physiotherapy at this stage involves:

  • Closed-chain exercises: mini squats, step-ups, stationary cycling
  • Proprioception work to restore balance and joint awareness
  • Hydrotherapy where appropriate, particularly for patients with higher BMI or bilateral knee issues

Patients who underwent a straightforward meniscectomy typically return to light desk work and comfortable walking distances by the end of the second month. Those who had a meniscal repair follow a more conservative and gradual programme.

Months 3 and 4: Functional Recovery

This phase is concerned with functional movement — walking longer distances, climbing stairs without support, and returning to low-impact activities such as swimming and cycling. At our clinic in Delhi, we coordinate directly with physiotherapy teams to ensure protocol continuity throughout this critical period.

Months 5 and 6: Return to Sport and Full Activity

Clearance for return to sport depends on the procedure performed. General timelines:

Procedure Typical Return to Sport
Meniscectomy4–6 weeks
Meniscal repair4–6 months
Chondroplasty2–3 months
ACL reconstruction9–12 months

Before returning to competitive activity, the operated knee should reach at least 90% of the strength of the unaffected knee. This benchmark exists for good reason — premature return is the leading cause of re-injury.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is knee arthroscopy a major surgery?
No. It is minimally invasive with small incisions and substantially less recovery time than open knee surgery. That said, the pathology being treated inside the joint can be complex, and the rehabilitation demands should not be underestimated.
What slows down recovery after knee arthroscopy?
The three most common factors that delay recovery or lead to re-injury are: skipping or under-attending physiotherapy, returning to activity before strength targets are met, and inadequate swelling management in the first two weeks.
Can knee problems return after arthroscopy?
Arthroscopy treats the damage that is present at the time of surgery but does not prevent future degeneration. Maintaining a healthy weight, building lower limb strength, and avoiding high-impact overloading significantly reduce the likelihood of recurrence.
